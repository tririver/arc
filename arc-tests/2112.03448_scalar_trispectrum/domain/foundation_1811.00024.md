# The Cosmological Bootstrap: Inflationary Correlators from Symmetries and Singularities

Paper: arXiv:1811.00024
Authors: Arkani-Hamed, Baumann, Lee, and Pimentel

## High-Value Summary

- Builds a bootstrap for late-time de Sitter and inflationary correlators by treating them as boundary observables constrained by conformal symmetry, regularity, and singularity/factorization structure rather than direct bulk time evolution.
- Derives the momentum-space constraints for contact and exchange four-point functions of conformally coupled scalars, then fixes the scalar-exchange correlator by demanding the absence of unphysical singularities and the correct physical poles/residues.
- Shows that spinning exchange correlators can be generated from the scalar solution with ladder-like spin-raising operators, while weight-shifting operators map conformally coupled =2 solutions to massless =3 solutions relevant for inflation.
- Constructs inflationary bispectra as slow-roll deformations of de Sitter four-point functions, reproducing standard single-field results and extending them to massive exchange, including arbitrary spin, with squeezed limits encoding particle mass and angular dependence.
- Identifies the flat-space scattering amplitude as the appropriate analytic continuation/limit of the de Sitter correlator and uses the total-energy singularity structure as a diagnostic of the UV completion and locality properties.

## Section Summaries

### 1 Time Without Time

The section motivates the paper’s bootstrap approach by framing cosmological correlators as boundary observables in de Sitter space, where time evolution is not directly accessible and consistency must be inferred from spatial correlations. It argues that, like AdS correlators or flat-space amplitudes, inflationary observables should be constrained by symmetry and singularity structure rather than detailed bulk dynamics. The section also outlines the paper’s roadmap: derive and classify de Sitter four-point functions, map them to spinning and massless cases, then perturb them into inflationary three-point functions.

### 2 Amplitudes Meet Cosmology

This section sets up the analogy between flat-space scattering amplitudes and de Sitter correlation functions: Lorentz invariance, locality, and unitarity constrain amplitudes, while conformal symmetry and singularity structure constrain inflationary correlators. It shows that contact terms correspond to analytic pieces or specific momentum poles, exchange diagrams factorize through lower-point amplitudes, and the de Sitter four-point function obeys differential equations that play the role of on-shell factorization conditions. The section also explains how the flat-space amplitude is recovered from the de Sitter correlator in a suitable high-energy limit, making the symmetry and singularity bootstrap connection precise.

### 3 De Sitter Four-Point Functions

This section studies four-point correlators of conformally coupled scalars in de Sitter space, covering both local contact interactions and tree-level exchange of massive scalars. Contact terms are fixed by conformal invariance and regularity, giving a finite set of momentum-space structures organized by powers of the total energy k_t, while exchange diagrams satisfy differential equations whose solutions encode particle production and reduce to the flat-space scattering amplitude in the k_t -> 0 limit. The section also emphasizes that the nature of the k_t singularity diagnoses UV completion: local field-theory completions produce branch cuts or poles, whereas sufficiently soft gravitational or stringy UV behavior can remove the singularity altogether.

### Exchange of Spinning Particles

This section extends the de Sitter four-point bootstrap from scalar exchange to exchange of particles with spin, showing that the spinning correlators can be built from the scalar-exchange solution using ladder-like operators. The answer is decomposed into helicity components with polarization sums fixed by conformal symmetry, and explicit solutions are obtained for spins 1 and 2 as well as for arbitrary spin, including the discrete series of (partially) massless cases. The resulting correlators contain the expected singularity structure for propagating modes while lower-helicity components appear as constraint pieces rather than independent degrees of freedom.

### 5 Massless External Fields

This section extends the bootstrap from conformally coupled external scalars to massless external scalars relevant for inflation by introducing weight-shifting operators that map the =2 four-point functions to =3 solutions. It shows how the massless correlator constraints can be simplified by these operators, how contact and exchange solutions are related to the =2 case for spins 0, 1, and 2, and outlines a general algorithm for higher-spin weight shifting. The section also identifies the longitudinal spin contribution needed for inflationary three-point functions and notes that disconnected pieces break conformal symmetry in a controlled way.

### 6 Inflationary Three-Point Functions

This section explains how inflationary bispectra arise as soft limits of de Sitter four-point functions when one external leg is evaluated on the slowly time-dependent inflationary background, with the symmetry breaking controlled by the slow-roll parameter. It shows that the leading scalar bispectrum reproduces Maldacena’s result up to local terms and a gauge-dependent contribution fixed by the spectral tilt, and it extends the construction to massive exchange, including spin-2, where the squeezed limit reveals the expected angular dependence and particle-production signal. In this way, inflationary three-point functions are organized by de Sitter data plus controlled slow-roll deformations.

### 7 Comments on Phenomenology

This section interprets inflationary correlators as a cosmological analogue of resonance spectroscopy: massive particle exchange produces oscillatory squeezed-limit signals that reveal the particle’s mass and spin, while the smoother part of the correlator captures the overall exchange shape. It then proposes a physically motivated basis for inflationary bispectra built from the simplest scalar-exchange four-point function plus contact terms, showing that arbitrary spin-exchange bispectra can be reconstructed from the soft limit of that exchange solution and local templates. The section closes by noting that weakly broken conformal symmetry keeps the signatures clean but small, so larger non-Gaussianity likely requires more strongly broken symmetry or different dynamics.

### 8 Conclusions and Outlook

The paper argues that inflationary correlators can be fixed by symmetries and singularity structure in a bootstrap-style approach, rather than by direct time-evolution calculations. It identifies the scalar four-point exchange correlator as the basic building block, with massless and spinning cases generated by weight-shifting and spin-raising operators, and emphasizes that cosmological correlators encode flat-space scattering amplitudes. The section closes by outlining future directions, including external spin, loop corrections, stronger symmetry breaking, positivity constraints, and new geometric structures.

### Appendix A Conformal Symmetry

This appendix derives the conformal symmetry constraints on three- and four-point functions in de Sitter space by relating the late-time wavefunction of the universe to correlators. It shows how the disconnected part of the four-point function is built from three-point functions, gives the relevant spinning two-point tensor structure, and then explicitly applies dilatation and special conformal transformations to obtain the scalar four-point conformal invariance condition. The derivation also explains how momentum conservation and the symmetry constraints reduce the result to the form used in the main text.

### Appendix B Singularity Structure

This appendix checks the singularity structure of tree-level exchange four-point functions from both the boundary correlator and the bulk integral, and finds that the two computations agree. On the boundary side, the Delta=2 series and homogeneous solutions are analyzed near the singular limits to identify the logarithmic behavior and match its coefficients. On the bulk side, the exchange integral is shown to satisfy a differential equation whose hypergeometric solution is fixed by regularity and by the expected singular limits, providing a consistency check on the main-text framework.
